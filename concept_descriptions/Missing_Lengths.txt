Missing lengths refer to a concept in geometry and trigonometry where one or more sides or dimensions of a geometric shape or figure are unknown. This concept is often encountered in various mathematical problems and exercises, especially in the fields of geometry, trigonometry, and algebra.

Understanding the concept of missing lengths requires a basic understanding of the properties and characteristics of different geometric shapes, such as triangles, rectangles, circles, etc. For instance, in a right-angled triangle, if two sides are known, the missing length (the third side) can be found using the Pythagorean theorem. This theorem, named after the ancient Greek mathematician Pythagoras, states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides. 

In other types of triangles, the Law of Sines or the Law of Cosines can be used to find missing lengths. The Law of Sines states that the ratio of the length of a side of a triangle to the sine of its opposite angle is the same for all three sides. On the other hand, the Law of Cosines, which is a generalization of the Pythagorean theorem, can be used to calculate a missing side of a triangle when the lengths of the other two sides and the size of the included angle are known.

In the case of quadrilaterals like rectangles and squares, if the length of one side and the area are known, the missing length can be found by dividing the area by the known side. For a square, knowing the length of one side is enough to find the missing lengths, as all sides in a square are equal.

In circles, the missing length could refer to the radius, diameter, or circumference. If the diameter is known, the radius can be found by dividing the diameter by two, and the circumference can be calculated using the formula πd (where d is the diameter).
